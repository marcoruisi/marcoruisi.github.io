Understanding a Problem Also Means Knowing Who Can Solve It.

Ownership

I bought a projector.

Connecting a Bluetooth speaker should have been simple.

It wasn't.

The volume behaved unpredictably.

No setting changed the outcome.

Trying different devices didn't help either.

At first I assumed I had overlooked something.

Then I stopped looking for settings and started following the problem.

I decompiled the Bluetooth module.

I reconstructed how Android handled AVRCP Absolute Volume.

I inspected the interoperability database.

I checked the device state through ADB.

Each step ruled out another possibility.

Eventually a pattern emerged.

The speaker was probably being treated as compatible with a Bluetooth feature it didn't fully support.

For the first time, I had a coherent explanation.

Then I discovered something equally important.

Even if the hypothesis was correct, there was nothing left for me to change.

The interoperability database belonged to a read-only APEX package.

Without root access or a modified firmware, the system couldn't be altered.

For a moment, that felt like failure.

Then I realized the objective had quietly changed.

I was no longer trying to solve the problem.

I was trying to understand who actually owned it.

Once that became clear, writing to the manufacturer was no longer a generic support request.

It became a technical report.

Not a complaint.

A reproducible hypothesis supported by evidence.

A few days later, the support team replied that the entire analysis had been forwarded to their developers for internal verification.

I still don't know whether the hypothesis will prove correct.

That isn't the interesting part.

The interesting part is realizing that understanding a system also means recognizing where your responsibility ends and someone else's begins.

Sometimes solving a problem means discovering that it no longer belongs to you.
